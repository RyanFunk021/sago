# PrimaTap — LinkedIn Post Series

---

## Post 1: The Hook

What if typing is the dumbest thing we ever invented?

Think about what happens when you have an idea. It exists in your mind as pure meaning — complete, immediate, already structured. Then you spend the next 30 seconds converting it into letters, one at a time, through a system designed 150 years ago for mechanical hammers hitting ink ribbons.

I've been working on something different. A communication system that skips letters entirely and goes straight from thought to meaning. 15 fundamental concepts. Finger chords. No alphabet required.

The early results are strange. It might be faster than typing. It might be faster than talking.

More soon.

## Post 2: The Deeper Thing

Here's the part that keeps me up at night.

Every language ever spoken is secretly a code for sounds. Braille is a code for letters which are codes for sounds. Even your internal monologue — the voice in your head — is probably running in a spoken language you learned before you could read.

But meaning exists before language. Babies have intentions, desires, concepts — before they have a single word. Pre-linguistic thought is real. We just don't have a way to transmit it directly.

What if we built one?

I'm designing a system where the fundamental units aren't letters or words — they're semantic primitives. SELF. OTHER. WANT. NOT. MOVE. ALIVE. 15 concepts that underlie all human communication across every language on earth.

You tap them as finger chords. The meaning transfers directly. No translation layer.

The question I can't stop asking: what kind of mind develops if this is your first language?

## Post 3: The Device

PrimaTap in plain terms:

A wearable glove with vibration motors on the fingertips. 4 fingers. 15 chord combinations. Each chord = a semantic primitive.

A hearing person speaks normally. The device converts their speech to text, maps it to primitives, and squeezes the deaf-blind user's fingers in the corresponding pattern. The user taps back in chords. The device converts those chords to speech. The hearing person hears it.

No interpreter. No Braille. No phonemes. No letters. Just meaning, moving directly between two people.

The same hardware works for deaf users (visual display), blind users (audio tones), non-verbal users, and — here's where it gets interesting — anyone who wants a faster, silent, private way to talk to a computer.

A trained user can communicate faster than typing. A fluent user approaches the speed of speech.

One system. Every population.

## Post 4: The Speed Problem

A stenographer types 225 words per minute.
A fast human speaker talks at 175 words per minute.
You type, what — 60? 80?

The gap between how fast you think and how fast you can express it — that's not a personal failure. That's a design flaw in the interface between your brain and the world.

PrimaTap chord input has a theoretical throughput ceiling above steno, because the vocabulary is smaller and every chord is a single simultaneous gesture. The most common concepts are the simplest chords — one finger, not four.

With audio as a training layer (tap a chord, hear a tone, build the muscle memory), early experiments suggest the learning curve is weeks, not years.

The goal isn't a faster keyboard. The goal is an interface fast enough that it stops feeling like an interface — where the gap between thinking and communicating disappears entirely.

## Post 5: Two Native Speakers

The assistive technology application is real and needed. But the most interesting thing about PrimaTap isn't what it does for deaf-blind communication.

It's what happens when two fluent users talk to each other.

No translation layer. No speech-to-text, no text-to-speech, no NLP mapping. Both people share the same primitive vocabulary and encoding. One person taps SELF+WANT+WATER. The other's fingers feel exactly that — not the English word "thirsty," not any word at all. The meaning transfers without passing through language.

Natural language conversation is lossy. Words are ambiguous. Grammar is irregular. Every exchange requires the listener to do inferential work just to recover what was meant. We're so used to this overhead we've stopped noticing it.

A PrimaTap-to-PrimaTap conversation has none of it. Fixed vocabulary. Deterministic encoding. Fidelity approaching 100% within the expressive range of the system.

Two people who think natively in semantic primitives, communicating at the speed of finger motion, without ambiguity or noise.

I genuinely don't know what they'd be capable of. I think we should find out.

## Post 6: The Baby Question

Here's where I'm going to lose some people. Stay with me.

Everything I've described — the finger chords, the semantic primitives, the speed, the fidelity — those are features of a communication device. Useful. Novel. Worth building.

But babies don't learn devices. They acquire languages. And whatever structure their first language has gets wired into the architecture of their thinking at the hardware level, before they have any choice about it.

A child whose first language is PrimaTap doesn't learn to translate thoughts into primitives. The primitives ARE the thoughts. No irregular verbs. No arbitrary exceptions. No ambiguity inherited from a system that evolved accidentally over millennia. Just compositional meaning, precisely structured, from the very beginning.

We have strong evidence that the language you think in shapes what you can think. Mathematicians who develop better notation solve problems that were previously intractable — not because they got smarter, but because their representational system got cleaner.

What does a mind look like that was built on a clean representational system from day one?

I don't know. That's the experiment.
